## Preamble

Research methodology is the discipline by which an institution distinguishes scholarly work from mere assertion. An institution without an articulated methodology may produce useful materials, but cannot meaningfully defend its work when challenged, cannot guide successive generations of researchers, and cannot demonstrate the conditions under which its conclusions hold.

This document is the World Protocol Academy's first articulated research methodology. It is presented as Version 0.1 — an initial draft, deliberately so labelled. The Academy's research practice is in active development. The methodology is presented to support that development, not to claim that the Academy currently meets every standard the document describes.

The document is internal. It is the framework against which the Academy's researchers, contracted authors, and reviewers undertake work. It is also the framework against which the Academic Advisory Board will assess the Academy's scholarly direction. It is not a public statement of compliance with any external research standard, and the Academy shall not represent it as such.

## Status of This Methodology

This is Version 0.1. The version number is deliberately conservative. The methodology will evolve through:

Annual review by the Director and the Academic Advisory Board.

Adjustments arising from operational experience.

Adjustments arising from external feedback when WPA work is reviewed by external academics.

Adjustments to align with evolving international scholarly practice in the relevant fields.

Earlier versions are archived but not active. The active version is identified in the document control table above. Material amendments require approval of the Director with consultation of the Academic Advisory Board.

## Scope

This methodology applies to all research and research-adjacent outputs produced under the World Protocol Academy imprint, including:

Monographs and books.

Journal articles and conference papers.

Working papers, policy briefs, case studies, protocol analyses, and benchmark methodologies (publication categories per Section 11).

Curriculum and training materials where they make scholarly claims.

Conference proceedings the Academy publishes.

Public reports and white papers.

The methodology applies regardless of whether the author is the Academy's Director, an Academy staff member, a contracted external author, or a collaborating researcher whose work the Academy publishes.

Substantive fields covered: protocol, diplomacy, public communication, security studies, and communication skills (the “WPA fields”). The methodology recognizes these fields differ in their evidentiary norms and accommodates that variation while maintaining common discipline on transparency, citation, and review.

## 1. Definition of WPA Research

### 1.1 What WPA research is

WPA research is systematic inquiry, conducted under documented method, producing reasoned conclusions supported by evidence that other competent persons can examine. It distinguishes itself from opinion, advocacy, marketing, and personal recollection by adherence to four conditions:

Question. The work addresses a defined question or set of questions, articulated in advance.

Method. The work follows a documented method appropriate to the question, allowing readers to assess how the conclusions were reached.

Evidence. Claims are supported by evidence drawn from sources another competent reader could examine; evidence is cited transparently.

Honesty. Limitations, uncertainties, and contrary evidence are acknowledged; the work does not overclaim what its evidence supports.

### 1.2 What WPA research is not

To establish boundaries by negation:

WPA research is not the same as practitioner experience or institutional memory. The Academy values both, but they are not research outputs without further work to systematize and document.

WPA research is not marketing material. Promotional language, claims of uniqueness, and superlative framings have no place in research outputs even when the underlying work is legitimately significant.

WPA research is not unverified compilation. Restating others' claims without verifying them, or assembling secondary material without checking primary sources, does not constitute research under this methodology.

WPA research is not certified by virtue of being published under the WPA imprint. The Academy's imprint indicates internal review and acceptance of the methodology; it does not equate to peer-reviewed status unless the work has actually undergone external peer review (see Section 8).

### 1.3 Field-specific notes

The WPA fields differ in their evidentiary norms and the methodology accommodates this:

Protocol — research often draws on published protocol regulations, diplomatic memoirs, treaty texts, ceremonial precedents, and observed practice. Source verification emphasizes textual fidelity to authoritative regulations and reliable accounts of practice.

Diplomacy — research draws on official documents, treaties, diplomatic correspondence (where declassified), memoirs, journalistic accounts, and academic studies. Source verification emphasizes corroboration across independent sources, particularly where memoirs or journalistic accounts may carry bias.

Public communication — research draws on communication theory literature, case studies of public events, content analysis, audience research, and applied communication scholarship. Source verification emphasizes methodological transparency in any quantitative or qualitative analysis.

Security studies — research draws on security policy documents, treaty texts, academic literature on strategic studies, terrorism studies, conflict analysis, and intelligence studies. Source verification emphasizes care with classified-derived material, attribution problems, and disinformation risks.

Communication skills — research draws on rhetorical theory, applied communication research, training literature, and observational data on practice. Source verification emphasizes the distinction between empirical claims and pedagogical recommendations.

## 2. Acceptable Source Types

WPA research draws on multiple types of sources, weighted differently according to the question being addressed and the field. The table below establishes the categories the Academy recognizes and the weight assigned to each. Authors are expected to apply judgment beyond the table; the table is the floor, not the ceiling.

| Source category | Weight | Use and limits |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Treaties, conventions, official protocol regulations | Primary, high | Quoted with article and clause; full citation including issuing body, date, official version. Authoritative for the legal or institutional framework. |
| Government and intergovernmental documents | Primary, high | Including ministerial protocol guides, MFA correspondence guides, UN documents, EU and Council of Europe documents. Cited with document number where applicable. |
| Peer-reviewed academic literature | Secondary, high | Journal articles, scholarly monographs, university press publications. Preferred where claims need theoretical grounding or empirical support. |
| Standard reference works | Secondary, medium-high | Established handbooks, encyclopedias of diplomacy, protocol manuals widely accepted in the field. Useful for orientation, less suitable as sole support for novel claims. |
| Diplomatic memoirs and practitioner accounts | Mixed | Valuable for first-hand observation; subject to memory, position, and self-presentation effects. Used with corroborating sources where possible. |
| Reputable journalism and reportage | Secondary, medium | Used for contemporary events; preference for established outlets with editorial standards. Multiple independent sources sought for contested events. |
| Conference papers and working papers | Secondary, medium | Useful for emerging research; weight reflects degree of peer review. |
| Court judgments and legal opinions | Primary or secondary, high | Cited with full case reference. Useful for legal aspects of diplomatic immunity, international law, similar matters. |
| Interviews conducted under WPA methodology | Primary, conditional | See Section 6 for protocol governing interview use, consent, anonymization, verification. |
| Academy founder's prior published works | Conditional | Cited under the same standards as other secondary sources. See Section 14 on relationship between WPA founder-authored works and external sources. |
| Wikipedia, blogs, social media | Generally not acceptable | Not used as primary support. May be acknowledged as the path by which a topic became known to the researcher, but the underlying primary or secondary source is what gets cited. |
| AI-generated content | Not a source | AI tools are workflow instruments, not citable authorities. See Section 7 on AI-assisted workflow disclosure. |

### 2.1 Source language and translation

Where a source exists in multiple languages, the original language version is the authoritative source. Translations are used for accessibility but cited with both original and translated reference where the distinction matters. For non-Macedonian and non-English sources where the researcher does not read the original language fluently, the translation used is identified and the limitation acknowledged.

### 2.2 Verification of secondary sources

When a secondary source quotes or paraphrases a primary source, the WPA author shall, where reasonably possible, consult the primary source directly. Citation chains where Source C cites Source B citing Source A are inspected for transmission errors. The Academy has direct experience with citation drift in its own materials and treats verification of secondary sources as a routine discipline, not an optional courtesy.

### 2.3 Currency of sources

Sources are evaluated for currency relative to the question. Foundational works in protocol and diplomacy may be authoritative for decades; treaty texts hold until amended; empirical claims about current practice require recent sources. Authors note when a source has been superseded by a later authoritative version.

## 3. Citation and Bibliography Standards

### 3.1 Citation system

WPA outputs use the author-date (Chicago author-date or APA-style) citation format as default. Author-year-page references appear in parentheses in the running text; full bibliographic details appear in a complete bibliography at the end of the work. Where a publication is intended for a journal or venue with a different style requirement (e.g., legal-style footnotes for a law journal), the venue's style governs.

### 3.2 Required citation elements

Every citation, regardless of style, includes sufficient information to allow another reader to retrieve the source. At minimum:

Author or institutional author.

Title of the work (article, chapter, book, document).

Year of publication.

Publisher or journal name with volume and issue.

Pages cited (or article-number for online journals).

DOI or persistent identifier where available.

URL and access date for online-only sources.

For treaties: name, parties, date of signature, date of entry into force, official source.

### 3.3 Quotations vs paraphrase

Direct quotations are reserved for cases where the exact wording is the substance of the point (statutory language, treaty articles, distinctive phrasing whose precise form matters). Routine reference to others' findings is paraphrased and cited.

Direct quotations of more than a sentence or two are reviewed for fair use compliance. Quotations of song lyrics, poetry, and other short creative works are not reproduced in full under any circumstance.

### 3.4 Self-citation

WPA authors who cite their own prior work treat that work under the same evidentiary standards as any other secondary source. Self-citation does not create a privileged status. See Section 14 for the specific case of the Academy founder's prior works.

### 3.5 No invented citations

Under no circumstance may a WPA author invent, fabricate, hallucinate, or guess at a citation. This is stated explicitly because AI tools, when used in drafting, sometimes produce confident-looking citations to works that do not exist or to pages that do not contain the cited content. Any citation appearing in a WPA output has been verified by the author against the actual source.

If a researcher cannot verify a citation, the cited claim is removed or replaced with an alternative formulation supported by a citation that is verified.

### 3.6 Bibliography

Each work includes a complete bibliography of sources cited. The bibliography is arranged alphabetically by author surname. Multiple works by the same author are arranged chronologically.

Sources consulted but not cited are not included in the bibliography. “Further reading” sections, if used, are clearly distinguished from the bibliography.

## 4. How Claims Are Verified

Verification is the discipline by which a draft claim becomes a sustainable claim. The verification process is documented for each piece of WPA work, even if briefly, so that successive reviewers can trace how conclusions were reached.

### 4.1 Levels of claim and corresponding verification

Different categories of claim require different verification effort.

Definitional claims (“Diplomatic immunity is...”) are verified against authoritative reference sources and the relevant treaty or legal instrument. Where multiple definitions exist, the variation is acknowledged and a primary definition is selected with reasoning.

Historical claims (“The Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations was concluded in 1961 and entered into force in 1964”) are verified against primary documents and at least one authoritative secondary source. Dates, names, and quantitative claims are checked against multiple sources.

Institutional claims (“The Macedonian protocol service operates under...”) are verified against current institutional documents, official websites, and recent secondary literature. Care is taken with claims about current practice, which may have changed since the most recent published source.

Evaluative claims (“This approach has proven effective in...”) require explicit evidentiary support. Generic praise without evidence is not used. Where the evaluation is the author's, this is signalled (“in this author's assessment”) and the basis is stated.

Counterfactual or comparative claims (“Without this protocol, the outcome would have been...”) are flagged as inferences and supported by the strongest evidence reasonably available, with limitations noted.

Empirical claims (“73 percent of Macedonian diplomatic correspondence follows...”) require explicit method, data, and date. If the author conducted the underlying study, the method is documented in the work or in a supporting appendix; if the data come from another source, that source is cited and its method is briefly described.

### 4.2 Verification log

For substantial works (monographs, major reports), authors maintain a verification log: a working document, separate from the publication, recording each significant claim and the sources used to verify it. The log is internal but available to the Director or Academic Advisory Board on request, particularly if a claim in a published work is later challenged.

### 4.3 Disputed and contested claims

Where the literature contains contested claims (multiple credible accounts of the same event, competing interpretations, ongoing scholarly debate), WPA work acknowledges the dispute. The author may take a position; the position is supported with reasoning and the dissenting view is acknowledged. Pretending consensus exists where it does not is a methodological failure.

### 4.4 Withdrawn or corrected claims

If a published WPA claim is later found to be incorrect, the procedure is:

The Director is notified.

The error is verified.

A correction is issued in writing, identifying the original claim, the corrected claim, and the source of the correction.

Where the original work continues in circulation, the correction is appended or, in the case of digital works, the work is updated with a clear changelog.

Where the error is significant, the matter is reviewed by the Academic Advisory Board to identify whether process improvements are warranted.

Honest correction strengthens institutional credibility. Concealment, retroactive editing without disclosure, or silence on identified errors are inconsistent with this methodology.

## 5. How Case Studies Are Built

Case study is a primary method in protocol and diplomacy research, where comparative or experimental designs are often impractical and the value of close examination of specific cases is high. WPA case studies are constructed under documented method to distinguish them from anecdote.

### 5.1 Case selection

Case selection is documented. Cases may be selected for:

Typicality — the case represents a common pattern.

Atypicality — the case is unusual and reveals something masked in typical cases.

Pivotality — the case had consequences disproportionate to its size.

Comparison — the case is one of a paired or larger set chosen to illustrate variation.

Access — the case is one for which adequate sources are available; this is acknowledged as a selection constraint, not a methodological virtue.

Whatever the basis, the selection is stated. Reader who is not told why a case was selected cannot assess the generalizability of conclusions drawn from it.

### 5.2 Case construction

A WPA case study is built from sources, not from memory. Even where the author has personal recollection of the events, the case study is constructed by reference to documents, contemporaneous accounts, official records, and verifiable secondary sources. Personal recollection is acknowledged as such, with its limits, and is not the sole basis for any factual claim.

### 5.3 Case structure

WPA case studies typically follow a structure that allows readers to assess them:

Context — the setting, period, parties involved, relevant prior history.

Question — what the case is being examined to investigate.

Evidence — the specific events, decisions, communications, or outcomes that constitute the case.

Analysis — the author's reading of the evidence in light of the question.

Limitations — what the case does and does not allow the reader to conclude.

Sources — full citation of materials used.

### 5.4 Sensitive cases

Where a case study involves recent events, named individuals, sensitive negotiations, or matters where parties may still hold strong positions:

The author considers whether identification of individuals or institutions is necessary for the analytical purpose. Where it is not, anonymization is applied.

Where identification is necessary, the author considers the right of named persons to fair representation.

Where the case is drawn from the author's personal professional experience, conflict of interest is declared per Section 10.

Where ongoing harm could result from publication, the case is deferred or restructured.

### 5.5 Anonymization

Anonymization, where applied, is meaningful. Replacing a name with an initial while retaining all other identifying details (country, position, date, distinctive circumstances) does not anonymize. Where anonymization is required, the author removes or generalizes identifying details such that the persons or institutions involved cannot be reasonably identified by readers familiar with the field.

## 6. How Interviews Are Used

Interviews — semi-structured or unstructured conversations with practitioners, scholars, or witnesses — can be a valuable source in WPA research. They are also high-risk: respondents may misremember, may have positions to defend, may speak in confidence. This section establishes the protocol governing interview use.

### 6.1 Pre-interview

The researcher prepares the interview purpose in writing.

Initial contact identifies the researcher, the project, the Academy, and the intended use of the interview content.

The interviewee is informed of: the project's nature, how their words may be used (named, paraphrased, anonymized), how the recording (if any) is stored, and their right to review and withdraw.

Consent is obtained in writing where the intended use is direct quotation or named attribution.

### 6.2 During the interview

The interview is conducted with respect for the interviewee's time and the limits they place on subjects.

Recording, if used, is with explicit consent. Note-taking without recording is acceptable.

Off-the-record statements are honored; the researcher does not use such statements as the basis for written claims.

Background statements (“not for attribution”) are honored and used only as confirmation of points already established by other sources.

### 6.3 Post-interview

A transcript or detailed notes are produced as soon as practicable.

Where the interviewee is to be quoted directly, the relevant passage is sent to them for confirmation. Their right to clarify is honored; their right to suppress an embarrassing but accurate quote is more limited and handled case-by-case in consultation with the Director.

The interviewee receives a copy of the work that uses their material before publication where reasonably possible.

### 6.4 Storage and retention

Interview recordings, transcripts, and notes are held under Tier I controls per WPA-GOV-QB-001 §7.

Access is limited to the researcher, the Director, and authorized reviewers.

Retention follows the project's needs and applicable data protection law; default retention is the working life of the project plus seven years.

Deletion at the interviewee's request is honored where consent for retention has not been given.

### 6.5 Use of interview material

Direct quotation is used sparingly and only where the exact wording matters.

Paraphrase is more common; the source is identified by interview reference (“Author's interview with [name or anonymized identifier], [date], [place]”).

Where the interviewee speaks for an institution, the institutional position is verified against the institution's published statements; private remarks at variance with public statements are handled with care.

Anonymized interview material, like anonymized case material, is meaningfully anonymized.

### 6.6 When interviews are not used

Interviews are not used to circumvent the verification standards of Section 4. An interviewee's claim about a verifiable fact is verified through standard sources before being treated as established. The interviewee's contribution is interpretive — what the events meant, why decisions were made, what was at stake — rather than ground truth on facts that documents can establish.

## 7. AI-Assisted Workflow Disclosure

The Academy uses AI-assisted tools (large language models, structured analytical tools, drafting and editing aids) in research and publication workflows. This section establishes the disciplines under which AI tools are used and the disclosures the Academy makes.

### 7.1 AI as workflow instrument, not source

AI tools are instruments. They are not sources. An AI-generated paragraph, however well written, is not a citable authority. AI-generated factual claims are not accepted as evidence. AI-generated citations are not accepted as citations until verified against the actual cited source.

### 7.2 Acceptable uses of AI in WPA research workflow

Drafting assistance — generating initial drafts the human author then revises substantially.

Editing assistance — improving prose, structure, and clarity of human-authored content.

Translation assistance — first-pass translation that human reviewers then verify.

Summarization — condensing long source material; the human researcher verifies the summary against the source.

Brainstorming — generating analytical angles, alternative framings, possible objections; the human researcher selects, develops, and verifies.

Format conversion — converting between document structures, applying citation styles, formatting bibliography.

Code and data work — analytical scripting, data formatting, validation routines.

Comparative analysis — generating side-by-side comparisons that a human reviewer assesses.

### 7.3 Unacceptable uses of AI in WPA research workflow

Producing content that is published without substantive human review and revision.

Generating citations that are not verified against the actual cited source.

Generating quotations attributed to real persons that the persons did not say.

Producing translations published without verification by a human reader competent in both languages.

Treating AI-generated factual claims as established facts without verification.

Generating content that misrepresents another person's views without explicit, accurate sourcing.

### 7.4 Disclosure

Each WPA publication includes a brief AI-use disclosure, appropriate to the work's substance:

If AI tools were used in drafting, this is acknowledged in a methodology note or acknowledgments section.

If AI tools generated initial structure, drafts, or analyses that were then revised by the human author, the use is described.

The specific tools used (model family, version where known) are recorded internally; the publication notes that AI tools were used without necessarily naming each tool.

Where AI tools were used substantially in the analytical work (not just drafting), the publication describes the role and the verification applied.

### 7.5 Verification standard for AI-assisted content

Content that originates with AI assistance is held to the same evidentiary standards as content that originates without it. The fact that a draft sentence came from an AI tool does not lower the standard for verifying its claims; the human author who carries the work to publication is responsible for every claim it contains.

In practical terms: if an author cannot verify a sentence in the work — by reference to a source the author has actually consulted — the sentence is removed or rewritten until it can be verified.

### 7.6 The Academy's own AI-tool history

The Academy has used AI tools, including large language models, in producing internal materials. These materials include the existing 2210-question Practice / Knowledge Bank (classified per WPA-GOV-QB-001 §1.2). Where AI assistance produced material now in active use, the material is reviewed against this methodology before being treated as authoritative for any purpose. Materials that have not yet been reviewed are not represented as research outputs of the Academy.

## 8. External Review and Peer Review Principles

### 8.1 Internal review

All WPA publications undergo internal review before release. Internal review is conducted by:

The Director or designated WPA staff member.

At least one second reader where the work is substantial (a monograph, a major report, a journal-submission paper).

Where the work touches a specialized area outside the immediate expertise of WPA staff, an external subject-matter consultant where reasonably possible.

Internal review verifies adherence to this methodology, identifies factual errors, assesses argumentation, and checks for clarity. It does not substitute for external peer review.

### 8.2 External peer review

External peer review is the discipline by which the wider scholarly community assesses a work's contribution and validity. The Academy does not currently operate an internal peer review system; external peer review of WPA work is sought through:

Submission of articles to peer-reviewed journals in the relevant fields.

Submission of papers to peer-reviewed conferences with refereed proceedings.

Engagement of external reviewers (academics in the field) for substantial monograph work, with reviewers paid honoraria where resources permit.

The Academy aspires to make peer-reviewed publication a regular practice rather than an occasional event.

### 8.3 What “peer-reviewed” means and does not mean

The Academy uses the term “peer-reviewed” only where the work has actually been reviewed by independent academic peers under a structured peer review process organized by a journal, publisher, or conference. The Academy does not describe internal review as peer review.

A WPA publication that has not undergone external peer review is described as such, not as “internally peer-reviewed” or under any phrasing that could mislead readers about its review status.

### 8.4 Reviewer selection and conduct

Where the Academy commissions external reviewers (for monographs, major reports, conference proceedings), reviewers are selected for:

Subject-matter expertise.

Independence from the author and the Academy (no current employment, no recent collaboration).

Standing in the relevant field.

Reviewer identities are confidential during the review unless an open-review model has been agreed in advance. Reviewers commit to honest, constructive critique. The Academy honors reviewer recommendations seriously even when they require substantial revision; it does not treat external review as a formality.

### 8.5 Conference proceedings review

Where the Academy publishes conference proceedings (such as the OPC conference series), the review process for accepted papers is documented and consistent. The Academy does not represent proceedings as peer-reviewed unless they have actually undergone double-blind peer review under documented procedures.

### 8.6 Self-review prohibition

An author does not review their own work for the purpose of attesting to peer review. The Director does not formally peer review work the Director has authored. Independent assessment is what makes review meaningful.

## 9. Data Protection Principles

Research that involves personal data is subject to data protection requirements under the Republic of North Macedonia's Law on Personal Data Protection and, where applicable, the European Union's General Data Protection Regulation. This methodology operationalizes data protection at the level of research practice.

### 9.1 Lawful basis

Each research project that involves personal data identifies its lawful basis for processing. The most common bases for academic research are:

Consent — explicit, informed, and documented consent of the data subject.

Legitimate interests — where the research serves a legitimate institutional purpose and processing is proportionate.

Public interest — where the research advances a defined public interest under applicable law.

The lawful basis is documented at the start of the project and reviewed if circumstances change.

### 9.2 Data minimization

Only the personal data needed for the research is collected. Convenience or completeness is not a justification for collecting more than is required. Where identifying information is not necessary for the analysis, anonymized or pseudonymized data is preferred.

### 9.3 Storage

Personal data collected for research is held under Tier I or Tier C controls per WPA-GOV-QB-001 §7, depending on sensitivity.

Access is restricted to the research team and the Director.

Where data is transferred between researchers (e.g., to an external collaborator), the transfer is encrypted and the recipient is bound by equivalent confidentiality obligations.

### 9.4 Retention

Personal data is retained only as long as needed for the research. Default retention is the working life of the project plus seven years for verification purposes; longer retention requires documented justification. Where the data subject has consented to a specified retention period, that period governs.

### 9.5 Anonymization in publication

Where research findings are published, identifying information is anonymized unless identification is necessary for the analysis and the data subject has consented to identification. Anonymization is meaningful (per Section 5.5).

### 9.6 Special category data

Research involving special category data (health, biometric, religious, political, sexual orientation, ethnic origin) is subject to elevated requirements. Such research requires explicit Director approval, may require external legal review, and proceeds only under documented protocol.

### 9.7 Data subject rights

Data subjects whose personal data is processed for WPA research retain rights under applicable law, including:

Right of access — to know what data is held about them.

Right to rectification — to correct inaccurate data.

Right to erasure — to have data deleted, subject to legitimate research exceptions.

Right to object — to processing for reasons related to their particular situation.

Requests are directed to the Custodian or, where the Custodian is unavailable, to the Director. Requests are responded to within the time limits of applicable law.

### 9.8 Coordination

Data protection in research coordinates with the Academy's broader data protection function (or designated equivalent) and with the Privacy Review Checklist (WPA-PRIV-SD-001).

## 10. Conflict of Interest Principles

Conflict of interest, declared honestly, does not necessarily disqualify an author or reviewer. Concealed conflict, however, damages credibility for the institution as well as the individual.

### 10.1 What constitutes a conflict of interest

Financial interest in a subject of the research (ownership, board membership, paid consultancy with an entity discussed).

Personal interest (the research is about the author's family, close associates, or institutions where the author holds positions).

Professional interest (the research advances or undermines the author's prior public positions in ways that affect interpretation).

Institutional interest (the research advances or undermines the Academy's commercial, reputational, or strategic interests).

Funding interest (the research is funded by a party with stake in the outcome).

### 10.2 Declaration

Authors disclose conflicts of interest at the start of any project they undertake under the WPA imprint.

Disclosures are documented in writing and held by the Director.

Where a conflict is identified during a project, disclosure is made promptly.

Substantive conflicts are noted in the published work itself, in a conflict of interest statement.

### 10.3 Founder-author conflicts

The Academy's Director is also a researcher. The Director's research carries an inherent institutional-interest dimension: the Academy is the imprint, the founder is the author, and the work supports both. This is not concealed; it is declared. Director-authored works carry an explicit acknowledgment of the dual role and are subject to internal review by a second reader where reasonably possible.

### 10.4 Recusal

Conflicts that cannot be managed by disclosure are managed by recusal. The conflicted person does not participate in the decision, review, or analysis where the conflict is material. The Director makes recusal determinations; where the Director is conflicted, the Academic Advisory Board's senior member acts in that role.

### 10.5 Funding disclosure

Where research is funded by an external party (grant, sponsorship, contracted work), the funding source is disclosed in the published work. Where the funder has rights to review or comment on the work pre-publication, the nature and limits of those rights are disclosed.

The Academy does not accept funding arrangements where the funder has the right to suppress, alter, or veto findings; such arrangements are inconsistent with research integrity and are declined.

## 11. Publication Categories

WPA publications are organized into the following categories. Each carries a defined character, length range, and review level. The Academy aims for clarity about what category a given work belongs to, so readers can calibrate expectations.

| Category | Typical length | Purpose and characteristic features |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Working paper | 5,000–15,000 words | Preliminary or provisional analytical work; intended to elicit feedback; explicitly labelled as not final; may be revised into other forms. |
| Policy brief | 1,500–3,000 words | Concise analysis directed at policy implications; structured around question, evidence, and recommendation; avoids extensive theoretical exposition. |
| Case study | 3,000–10,000 words | Examined under Section 5 method; built around a defined case with clear question, evidence, analysis, and limitations. |
| Protocol analysis | 5,000–15,000 words | Detailed analysis of a specific protocol regulation, ceremonial practice, or procedural framework; includes textual analysis and comparative or contextual elements. |
| Benchmark methodology | 10,000–30,000 words | Methodological document describing how a comparative or evaluative measurement is constructed; includes definitions, criteria, scoring approach, validation, and limitations. Distinct from the published benchmark itself, which is a separate output. |

### 11.1 Larger forms

Beyond the categories above, the Academy may publish:

Monographs — book-length works, typically 50,000–120,000 words, often the integration of multiple papers and case studies. Subject to the most extensive review process.

Edited volumes — collections of contributed chapters under a thematic editor. Each chapter undergoes review; the editor takes responsibility for the volume's coherence.

Conference proceedings — collected refereed papers from Academy-organized or co-organized events.

### 11.2 Series naming and numbering

Each category has a consistent identifier. A working paper might be “WPA Working Paper 2026-04”; a policy brief “WPA Policy Brief 2026-12”. Identifiers are sequential within category and year, allowing citation and tracking.

### 11.3 Review level by category

Working papers — internal review by the Director or designated reader; explicit “draft for discussion” framing.

Policy briefs — internal review plus one second reader.

Case studies — internal review plus second reader; external consultation where the case is sensitive or specialized.

Protocol analyses — internal review plus second reader; external consultation where the analysis involves a foreign jurisdiction's protocol.

Benchmark methodologies — internal review plus second reader plus external review by at least one academic expert in measurement methodology before publication.

Monographs — internal review plus external review by at least two academic experts before publication.

## 12. Limits of WPA Certification Claims

This section is short by design. Its substance is that the Academy does not overstate what its certifications mean.

### 12.1 What the Academy may claim

That a person has completed a specified WPA program of study or training.

That a person has achieved a specified standard on a WPA assessment, where the assessment is part of a documented certification process.

That a publication has been issued under the WPA imprint, indicating internal review and acceptance under this methodology.

### 12.2 What the Academy does not claim

That a WPA certification is equivalent to state-recognized academic accreditation in the Republic of North Macedonia or any other jurisdiction, unless such equivalence has been formally established by the relevant accrediting authority.

That a WPA certification confers professional licensure in fields where licensure is regulated by other bodies.

That WPA certifications are recognized by named institutions, governments, or organizations, unless such recognition has been formally agreed and documented.

That a WPA certification is internationally recognized in any binding sense, unless such recognition has actually been earned through documented mutual recognition agreements.

### 12.3 Public communication discipline

Public communications about WPA certifications, training, and educational offerings shall be accurate. Marketing material, public website content, and certification documentation use language that accurately describes what the certification represents and does not imply standing it has not been earned. Where ambiguity exists, the Academy errs toward modest and accurate framing.

### 12.4 Aspirations

The Academy may aspire to formal accreditation, mutual recognition, or other forms of external validation. Such aspirations are pursued through documented work and are reported on as work in progress, not asserted as already achieved. Where the Academy is in dialogue with an accrediting body, the dialogue is described accurately, not advanced as accreditation already obtained.

## 13. Language and Translation Quality Rules

The Academy operates in a multilingual environment. Macedonian is a primary working language; English is the primary language for international scholarly engagement; other languages may be used for specific audiences and projects. Quality across languages is a methodological concern.

### 13.1 Primary language and target audience

Each WPA publication identifies its primary language and target audience. A monograph in Macedonian addressed to Macedonian protocol practitioners has different conventions from an English-language journal article addressed to international scholars in security studies. Authors and editors calibrate accordingly.

### 13.2 Internal Macedonian quality

Macedonian-language WPA publications are produced in standard literary Macedonian, with accepted scholarly register. Code-mixing with English (using English terms where Macedonian equivalents exist or could be coined) is minimized. Where a technical English term lacks an established Macedonian equivalent and translation would obscure rather than clarify, the English term is retained, glossed at first use, and used consistently.

### 13.3 English-language quality

English-language WPA publications are produced in standard scholarly English. Where the author's first language is not English, the work is reviewed by a competent English-language editor before submission to English-language venues.

### 13.4 Translation

When a WPA publication is translated, translation is treated as a substantive task, not a mechanical one. Quality requirements:

Translation is performed by a translator competent in both the source language and the target language.

Where AI tools are used in first-pass translation, a competent human translator reviews and revises the output before publication.

Technical and field-specific terminology is verified against established conventions in the target language.

Citations and quoted material in the original language are checked for accurate transmission.

Translated works carry a translator credit and an acknowledgment of any AI assistance used in the translation process.

### 13.5 Multilingual outputs

Where a single output appears in multiple languages, the relationship between versions is documented. If one version is the original and others are translations, this is stated. If versions have been developed in parallel, this is stated. Inconsistencies between versions are not allowed; either they are resolved before publication or the discrepancy is acknowledged with reasoning.

### 13.6 Macedonian as institutional voice

Where the Academy speaks institutionally — through its public website, official statements, conference materials — Macedonian is the primary voice with English alongside. The Macedonian text is not a translation of the English; it is an original institutional voice in its own right. This reflects the Academy's location, audience, and identity.

## 14. Relationship Between WPA Founder-Authored Works and External Sources

The Academy's Director is the author of a body of works in protocol, diplomacy, and related fields. These works pre-date the Academy in some cases and have been produced under various imprints. The relationship between the founder's works and external scholarly sources requires explicit treatment.

### 14.1 Founder-authored works as sources

Founder-authored works are cited under the same standards as any other secondary source (Section 2). They do not have privileged authority within WPA outputs.

### 14.2 Avoidance of citation circularity

Citation circularity (the founder's work cited as authority for claims that originate in that work, with no external corroboration) is identified and avoided. Where a claim appears in a founder-authored work, the WPA author asks: what is the underlying source for this claim? The underlying source is what gets cited; the founder-authored work is acknowledged as the path to the source where appropriate.

### 14.3 Founder-authored works that lack external corroboration

Where a claim appears only in founder-authored works and cannot be corroborated by external sources, the claim is treated as the founder's analytical or interpretive contribution, not as established fact. WPA outputs that rely on such claims acknowledge them as such.

### 14.4 Engagement with external scholarship

WPA work engages with the external scholarly literature in the relevant fields. The Academy's intellectual position is developed in dialogue with — not in isolation from — international scholarship in protocol, diplomacy, public communication, security studies, and communication skills. Authors are expected to have read and to engage with relevant external literature, including literature that disagrees with positions the Academy or its founder has previously advanced.

### 14.5 Doctrinal contributions

The Academy and its founder may make doctrinal contributions — characteristic positions, frameworks, formulations associated with the Academy. Doctrinal contributions are presented as such, with attribution, and supported by reasoning. They are not represented as universally accepted views, established orthodoxy, or scholarly consensus where they are not.

### 14.6 Practical example

A WPA author writing on diplomatic protocol may cite the founder's monographs alongside Berridge's Diplomacy: Theory and Practice, Wood and Serres' Diplomatic Ceremonial and Protocol, and other established works. The founder's contribution stands alongside other contributions on its merits, supported by reasoning, and the reader is given enough citation context to assess each.

## 15. Honest Framing

### 15.1 No overclaiming

WPA publications avoid overclaiming. Specific patterns to avoid:

“World-class,” “world-leading,” “world reference,” “world's first,” “world-unique” and similar superlative framings.

Implications that the Academy holds standing it has not earned (peer-reviewed, accredited, internationally recognized) where such standing has not been documented.

Generic praise framings (“groundbreaking,” “innovative,” “revolutionary”) applied to the Academy's own work.

Suggestions of consensus, recognition, or acceptance that have not been earned.

Preferred alternatives are accurate descriptions: “specialized institute,” “regional reference,” “growing international platform,” “Institute for Protocol, Diplomacy, Public Communication & Security Studies.” Truth is durable; overclaiming damages credibility when scrutinized.

### 15.2 No false accreditation claims

WPA publications do not claim accreditation, recognition, or institutional standing the Academy has not actually earned. Specifically:

The Academy is not a state-recognized higher education institution unless and until such recognition is formally granted by the Republic of North Macedonia's Board of Accreditation and Evaluation of Higher Education.

WPA certifications are not equivalent to academic degrees unless such equivalence is formally established.

The Academy's outputs are not peer-reviewed unless they have actually undergone external peer review.

Aspirations toward formal accreditation are described as aspirations, not as standing already achieved.

### 15.3 Honest gap acknowledgment

Where the Academy's actual research practice falls short of this methodology, the gap is acknowledged internally. Documents like this methodology describe the standard the Academy is committed to operating under; if specific outputs do not yet meet the standard, this is recognized rather than concealed.

Honest gap acknowledgment is itself a methodological discipline. An institute that claims discipline it does not have damages itself when the gap is exposed; an institute that documents the gap and the work to close it builds credibility over time.

### 15.4 Critical engagement with own work

This methodology applies to the Academy's own outputs, including outputs already in circulation. Materials produced before this methodology was articulated may not fully conform to it. The Academy commits to:

Applying the methodology to all new outputs.

Reviewing existing outputs against the methodology when they are revised, reissued, or cited as authority.

Acknowledging where existing outputs fall short, rather than claiming retroactive compliance.

### 15.5 Public communication consistency

The discipline of this section governs not only publications but also the Academy's public communications: the website, marketing material, conference promotional content, and any external statements about the Academy's standing, achievements, and recognition. The Academy's public voice and its scholarly voice are held to consistent standards.

### 15.6 Closing principle

Credibility is earned through visible adherence to clear standards over time. It is not earned through marketing language, claimed standing, or assertion. This methodology exists to support the earning of credibility — by establishing the standards, by guiding the work, and by holding the Academy's outputs accountable to what they say they are.
